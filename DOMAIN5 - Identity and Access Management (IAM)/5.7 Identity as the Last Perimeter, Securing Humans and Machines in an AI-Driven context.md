## 5.7.1 Evolution of the perimeter ##

In traditional security models, the network perimeter was the primary line of defense. That model is no longer sufficient. In an AI-driven environment, where workloads are distributed, cloud-native, and increasingly autonomous, identity becomes the new perimeter. It is no longer just about who accesses a system, but also about what accesses it.

Identity and Access Management evolves to address a fundamental shift: identities are no longer limited to human users. AI agents, automated service accounts, APIs, and machine-to-machine interactions now dominate modern environments. These non-human identities operate at scale, often making decisions and accessing resources without direct human intervention.

## 5.7.2 Least Privilege and Privilege escalation in the AI era

A critical concept is the Principle of Least Privilege. `Every identity, whether human or machine, must be granted only the minimum level of access required to perform its function`. This becomes particularly important with AI systems. Unlike traditional applications, AI agents may dynamically interact with multiple datasets, services, and environments during both training and execution. 

> ⚠ Without strict privilege boundaries, an AI system could inadvertently or maliciously access sensitive data beyond its intended scope.

**Privilege escalation** is one of the most significant risks in this space. `If an AI agent is granted excessive permissions, or if its access controls are poorly defined, it may expand its reach across systems`. This is not always the result of an external attack. It can occur due to flawed design, misconfigured roles, or unintended behavior during model execution. 

To mitigate this risk, CISSP practitioners must treat non-human identities with the same rigor applied to human users. This includes strong authentication mechanisms, lifecycle management, regular auditing, and continuous monitoring. Service accounts should not be static or overprivileged. Instead, they should be dynamically managed, time-bound where possible, and tightly scoped to specific tasks. Identity governance must extend beyond users to include every entity that interacts with the system.

## 5.7.3 AI enabling Identity Management ##

AI is not only a risk factor—it is also a powerful enabler of stronger Identity and Access Management. One of the most impactful applications is in behavioral biometrics and adaptive authentication. Traditional authentication methods rely on static credentials such as passwords or tokens. These methods are increasingly vulnerable to compromise. AI introduces a dynamic layer by analyzing how users behave rather than just what they know.

Behavioral biometrics examine patterns such as typing speed, mouse movement, login times, device usage, and geographic location. By building a baseline of normal behavior, AI systems can detect anomalies in real time. For instance, if a user who typically logs in from Italy during business hours suddenly attempts access from another region at an unusual time, the system can flag this as suspicious. Instead of granting or denying access outright, adaptive authentication adjusts the level of verification required. The user might be prompted for additional factors, or access may be restricted until further validation is completed.

This approach transforms IAM from a static gatekeeper into a dynamic, risk-aware system. Access decisions are no longer binary but contextual. They consider not only identity but also behavior, environment, and risk level. This significantly enhances security without unnecessarily burdening legitimate users.

The integration of AI into IAM also improves **scalability**. As organizations grow and systems become more complex, manually managing identities and access rights becomes impractical. AI-driven systems can analyze large volumes of access data, identify patterns, and recommend optimizations. They can detect dormant accounts, excessive permissions, and unusual access paths that would otherwise go unnoticed. 

> ⚠ Organizations must secure the identities of AI systems while simultaneously trusting AI to enhance security decisions. This requires careful design, transparency, and governance. AI models used in IAM must be explainable, auditable, and resistant to manipulation. If an attacker can influence the behavior of an AI-driven authentication system, they may be able to bypass controls or generate false trust signals.

**Identity is no longer a static attribute; it is a dynamic and continuously assessed condition.**
